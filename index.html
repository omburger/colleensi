About Structural Integration

What is the Rolf Method of Structural Integration?

The Rolf Method of Structural Integration is a form of bodywork that involves changing the structure, function, and energy of the body to bring the body into its natural state of alignment and flexibility. It uses deep muscular manipulation and guided movements to release patterns of emotional and physical strain within the fascia, or connective tissue, of the body.


The fascia, rather like the membranes around the segments of an orange, wraps the muscles and gives them their shape. It is meant to be fluid and flexible, but it can shorten, thicken, or adhere in response to emotional or physical trauma, habitual postures, repetitive movements, attitudes, and beliefs. This sets up a progression of compensation patterns and distortions that build upon themselves. Structural Integration works to bring fascia back to its natural flexibility, altering the shape of muscles so new patterns of movement can be created.


Rather than relating to the body as a set of parts to be fixed, Structural Integration emphasizes the interdependence of the parts of the body, each supporting and balancing the rest. In a Structural Integration session, both practitioner and client attempt to remain fully present during the session, so that the practitioner can facilitate the client accessing her own inherent body wisdom to create awareness for pattern shifts, which helps physical changes become lasting ones.


The Rolf Method of Structural Integration was developed by Ida Rolf, a biochemist and physiologist whose work revolutionized the use of the words “core” and “sleeve” to describe the relationship of the body to reality and gravity. Muscles and body segments are differentiated and then re-integrated in relationship to each other in a way that evokes a sense of core and allows movement to flow through it in the most efficient, harmonious, and stress-free manner possible.


Process

Typically, people go through a ten-session series that progressively addresses deeper layers of the body. Sessions are about 75 minutes long and are spaced usually from one to two weeks apart. It isn't necessary to commit to all ten sessions at the outset; you can experience changes in one session. However, the results of Structural Integration are cumulative, and each session builds upon the results from the previous one.


Structural Integration work can be quite deep, but it can also be very subtle and sometimes touch may even be barely perceptible, working with the breath and the fluid craniosacral pulse. The intensity of an intervention is defined by the client’s comfort zone and works with the nervous system to find the pace and depth that allows optimum release.

Benefits

People of all ages and types can benefit from Structural Integration. This work can successfully be used for improving posture, athletic performance, and flexibility, as well as addressing chronic aches and pains that have not responded to other treatments. It can be part of a program to optimize well-being or used to help relieve old traumas locked in the physical structure. After a Structural Integration session, typical reactions are feelings of spaciousness, lightness, less aches and pains, or being taller or straighter. Increased body awareness over time allows the body to better self-organize, which ultimately sustains the changes.
